Designing TeachAI for teachers and coaches

Shaun Herron · Senior Product Designer

TeachAI is a design concept for schools: help teachers get useful feedback sooner, and help coaches support more people without drowning in work. Two linked apps (one for teachers, one for coaches), built as clickable web demos.

Unified case study (recommended) Artifact edition Context My Role Process Artifacts

Context

Teachers often wait too long for feedback, and when it comes, it may not match their next class. Coaches can’t be in every room. This project asks: can software help turn lesson videos and materials into useful notes fast, while coaches still approve what gets sent?

I worked with product leadership and a PM, and showed drafts to stakeholders and school-side contacts to keep the language clear and trustworthy.

The tension

Slow or vague feedback

Coaches are stretched thin; teachers need timely, specific help.
The idea

AI assists, people decide

Software drafts; coaches edit and send; teachers choose how to respond.
What we built

Two linked apps

Teacher experience + coach dashboard, same visual language.

My Role

  • Research: Interviews and synthesis with product leadership and the PM to agree on problems and what “good” looks like.
  • Existing tools: Looked at older products so the new design fits real habits.
  • Design: Maps and screens for full teacher and coach journeys.
  • Visual system: Shared look and components so both apps feel like one product.
  • AI wording: Iterated with the PM so suggestions sound like a coach, not a chatbot.
  • Feedback: Showed work to stakeholders and school contacts; fixed what confused them.

The demos are static HTML pages you can open in a browser (no server required), so anyone can click through the flow.

How I approached the work

1

Agree on the problem with PM and leadership

Got on the same page about coach workload, teacher needs, and what counts as useful feedback, before drawing screens.

2

Map how people work today

Reviewed older tools so we didn’t design something that ignores day-to-day habits.

3

Sketch flows, then a shared look

Rough screens first, then colors, type, and reusable pieces for both teacher and coach.

4

Tune the AI text and review screens

Adjusted wording and layout so suggestions feel professional and easy for coaches to edit before send.

5

Test with stakeholders and school contacts

Checked for clarity, trust, and “what do I do next?” for both teachers and coaches.
