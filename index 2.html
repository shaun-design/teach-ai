Case study · AI-assisted feedback system

TeachAI: Reducing feedback time from days to minutes with AI + human review

Teachers often wait days for feedback they can’t use in their next class. Coaches are stretched across dozens of teachers and can’t support everyone deeply.

I designed a system where AI generates structured feedback in minutes, and coaches review, edit, and send it, reducing turnaround time while maintaining trust.
Designed to reduce feedback turnaround from ~3 to 5 days to same-day.

Problem

Feedback is delayed, generic, and disconnected from upcoming lessons.

Approach

AI-generated drafts + mandatory coach review to balance speed and trust.

My Role

Partnered with product on research and synthesis; led product design, UX/UI, and built interactive prototypes.

Overview

AI product exploration for an existing platform

This work was an internal product exploration to identify how AI could be introduced into an existing education platform. It was initiated by company leadership to evaluate opportunities, risks, and potential product directions.

The outcome is a set of product concepts, system designs, and interactive prototypes that demonstrate how AI-assisted feedback could reduce turnaround time while maintaining trust and quality.

My Role
Senior Product Designer. Partnered with product leadership to explore AI opportunities, contributed to research and synthesis, defined product direction, designed end-to-end experiences, and built interactive prototypes to evaluate concepts.
Who I worked with
Product leadership, a product manager, and engineering partners (for a future real build).
How long
Several phases over a few weeks: learn the problem, sketch ideas, refine the design, get feedback, repeat. Exact dates are illustrative.
What shipped here
Product concepts, system-level design explorations, and interactive prototypes used to evaluate how AI could be integrated into the platform.
Exploration work intended to inform product direction; not all concepts were implemented.

The Problem

Feedback arrives too late to change what happens in the classroom

Feedback cycles break when they are too slow to influence the next lesson. What should support real-time improvement instead becomes reflection after the fact.

Coaches can’t be in every classroom, and feedback depends on scheduling. As a result, feedback often arrives days later, missing the moment when it would be most useful.

Coaches are also responsible for many teachers at once, making it difficult to provide deep, consistent support. When tools introduce extra steps or complexity, they are quickly ignored in favor of speed.

This creates a gap: feedback is no longer actionable. Teachers receive general advice disconnected from their immediate context, limiting its impact on instruction.

The opportunity is not just to generate more feedback, but to make it usable in time. This requires balancing speed with trust, automating the heavy lifting while keeping coaches in control of what is ultimately shared.

Our approach is to use AI to generate structured feedback from lesson artifacts, while requiring coach review before anything is sent. This enables faster turnaround without sacrificing quality or professional judgment.

Users & Research

How feedback works today and where it breaks down.

Participants

We interviewed 6 teachers, 6 instructional coaches, and 4 school leaders to understand workflows across the full coaching cycle.

Participants included both heavy users and low to mid adoption users. Heavy users regularly engaged with video feedback and coaching workflows, while lower adoption users revealed key friction points and barriers to use.

We reached saturation within each group, with consistent patterns around feedback timing, prioritization, and trust in AI-generated insights.

So we focused on: feedback that fits the next lesson, not a pile of data.

Constraints

  • It had to fit into busy school schedules.
  • Feedback had to be available quickly enough to be used in the next lesson.
  • Coaches had to review and approve all feedback before it was shared.
  • The system had to work for both frequent users and low-adoption users.
  • The existing product was built around video and could not be removed entirely.
  • The system had to be fast, usable, and trustworthy to support teaching in the moment.

Tradeoff

Speed vs. depth

Fast, rough feedback can miss nuance. Slow, perfect feedback never ships. We leaned toward fast enough to use, with the coach as the safety check.

Faster notesMore detail

The core product tension was between speed and depth. Faster feedback risks losing nuance, while deeper feedback risks arriving too late to be useful.

Where we landed: show the summary and key takeaways first; video and details support that, not the other way around.

Key Decision

These insights directly informed a set of key product decisions that shaped how feedback is delivered, prioritized, and trusted within the system.

Summary-first feedback instead of video-first

ProblemTeachers were not engaging with full lesson recordings, limiting the effectiveness of video-based feedback.
Options
  • Video-first interface
  • Enhanced video navigation
  • Summary-first approach
DecisionLead with a structured summary, with video as supporting evidence.
WhyTeachers need immediate, actionable insight due to time constraints and cognitive load.
ResultIncreased engagement with feedback and made it easier for teachers to apply insights in their next class.

Prioritization over visibility

ProblemCoaches had limited time and could not review all teachers equally.
Options
  • Show all data equally
  • Allow manual filtering
  • Automatically prioritize teachers
DecisionSurface a prioritized list of teachers based on need.
WhyCoaches needed help deciding where to focus more than accessing more data.
ResultReduced cognitive load and enabled faster, more effective coaching decisions.

Structured AI with human control

ProblemEarly AI-generated feedback sounded generic and sometimes too formal, which reduced trust.
Options
  • Keep AI output as-is
  • Let coaches fully rewrite everything
  • Constrain AI to structured, editable drafts
DecisionUse AI to generate structured, editable drafts with clear sections such as summary, strengths, and next steps.
WhyCoaches needed speed without losing ownership or trust.
ResultFeedback became more consistent, easier to review, and faster to deliver.
This established a core principle: the system should accelerate decision-making while keeping coaches in control of what is ultimately shared.

Edge Cases & Safeguards

Designed to handle uncertainty, time pressure, and varying levels of trust in AI.

AI trust and control

If AI is wrong
  • Coach can fully edit or discard suggestions
  • Nothing is sent without human review
If users trust AI too much
  • AI output is presented as a draft, not a final answer
  • Coach review remains required
If users do not trust AI
  • Clear distinction between AI-generated and coach-approved feedback
  • Human review is visible in the workflow

Workflow and time constraints

If a coach has no time
  • Dashboard prioritizes highest-need teachers
  • Quick-edit flows reduce time to send feedback
If a coach has too many teachers
  • The system surfaces who needs attention first
  • Lower-priority items do not compete for focus

Input quality and context

If context is missing
  • Artifacts such as lesson plans or student work are shown alongside feedback
  • AI output is framed as partial, not complete
If the input is low quality
  • Coach can ignore or adjust suggestions
  • The system avoids overstating confidence

Adoption and behavior

If a teacher disagrees
  • Feedback is framed as suggestions, not evaluation
  • Encourages reflection and follow-up conversation
If feedback is hard to act on
  • Next steps are clear, specific, and easy to apply
  • Designed to fit within a single lesson cycle

Iterating on Feedback

We treated this as an iterative process, using feedback from users and stakeholders to refine the experience over time. Each round of input helped clarify priorities, improve usability, and strengthen trust in the AI-driven workflow.

This approach ensured the product evolved based on real needs rather than assumptions, leading to a more focused and effective experience.

Results

What we would measure

Success is not just faster feedback. It is feedback that is trusted and acted on.

We measure success across three areas, each tied to a key product decision.

Speed

Decision: Deliver feedback immediately with AI instead of requiring full video review

  • Time from lesson submission to feedback delivered
  • Time from feedback received to changes applied in the classroom

↓ Target: same-day feedback cycle

Trust & quality

Decision: Ensure AI feedback is reviewed and edited by coaches before sharing

  • % of AI feedback reviewed and edited by coaches
  • Average number of edits per feedback draft
Adoption & use

Decision: Prioritize next steps and make feedback easy to access and act on

  • % of feedback viewed within 24 to 48 hours
  • Teacher rating: Was this useful for your next lesson

↓ Signal: most teachers act within one teaching cycle

These metrics ensure the system is fast, trusted, and actionable within real teaching workflows.

Theory we’re testing

If feedback arrives quickly and feels specific, teachers apply it sooner, leading to faster improvement than traditional coaching cycles.

Reflections

Principles I Would Carry Forward

  • Speed only matters if people trust the output. AI can reduce effort, but without clear human control, it won’t be used.
  • Prioritization is more valuable than more data. Coaches didn’t need more information. They needed to know where to act first.
  • AI products require visible boundaries. It must be clear what AI generated vs. what a coach approved.
  • Designing for real workflows beats ideal workflows. Teachers and coaches work under time pressure, so every extra step risks abandonment.
  • AI outputs improve when inputs are well defined. Well-structured inputs and aligned goals lead to more useful and reliable results.
